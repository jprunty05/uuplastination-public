1. What is plastination?

Plastination is a preservation technique that replaces the water and fat in biological tissues with durable polymers such as silicone, epoxy, or polyester resin. The result is a dry, odorless, and long-lasting specimen that can be handled and studied without decay.

2. Who invented plastination?

Plastination was developed in 1977 by Dr. Gunther von Hagens at Heidelberg University in Germany. His method revolutionized anatomical preservation and education worldwide.

3. How does the plastination process work?

Plastination generally involves four main steps:

  1. Fixation ‚Äì The specimen is treated with formaldehyde to halt decomposition.
  2. Dehydration ‚Äì Water and lipids are removed using acetone.
  3. Forced Impregnation ‚Äì Acetone is replaced with a liquid polymer under vacuum.
  4. Curing ‚Äì The polymer is hardened with gas, heat, or UV light to create a stable, permanent specimen.
4. Why is plastination important for education and research?

Plastinated specimens maintain anatomical accuracy while being safe, clean, and reusable. They allow students and researchers to study real biological structures in detail without the health and ethical concerns associated with traditional cadaver preservation.

5. What types of specimens can be plastinated?

Almost any biological specimen can be plastinated ‚Äî from whole organs and body parts to entire animal specimens, tissues, and even plant samples. At Union University‚Äôs Plastination Lab, we focus on educational specimens that support teaching in anatomy, physiology, and biology courses.

6. How long does plastination take?

Depending on the size and complexity of the specimen, the process can take anywhere from a few weeks to several months. The dehydration and impregnation stages are the most time-intensive.

7. What materials are used in plastination?

Key materials include acetone, silicone polymer, curing agents, and vacuum equipment. We also use specialized flow-control valves, temperature sensors, and vacuum pumps to regulate the process.

8. Is plastination safe?

When performed in a controlled environment with proper ventilation and safety equipment, plastination is safe. The finished specimens are completely non-toxic and can be handled without gloves.

9. Can the public view plastinated specimens?

Yes. Depending on current projects and privacy considerations, the lab occasionally hosts open houses, academic tours, and student showcases to display plastinated models and explain the process.
